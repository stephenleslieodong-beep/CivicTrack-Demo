Final Instructions – Preserve the Architecture, Enrich the Experience
I completely agree with your architectural reasoning.

You are absolutely right that bolting dozens of unrelated features into a single HTML file would undermine the discipline this project has maintained from the beginning: a single shared stylesheet, a consistent design language, a coherent information architecture, reusable components, and a codebase that remains understandable, maintainable and traceable.

I would much rather have a polished, professional, government-grade platform than a sprawling prototype filled with disconnected demonstrations.

That said, I want us to make one important distinction.

If a feature is unrelated to Uganda's National Development Plan IV (NDP IV), the government planning process, public service delivery, monitoring and evaluation, district performance, or parliamentary oversight, then remove it without hesitation. Do not keep anything simply because it looks impressive.

However, if a feature genuinely supports the objectives of NDP IV—even indirectly—it should remain and be strengthened.

For example:

An MP should never appear to personally finance district projects. That would misrepresent the role of Parliament.

Instead, MPs should be able to:

follow public expenditure

monitor budget releases

compare planned versus actual implementation

identify stalled projects

identify implementation bottlenecks

monitor accountability

monitor service delivery

engage responsible government agencies

monitor district performance over time

Their role is oversight, coordination, advocacy and accountability—not direct financing.

The platform should consistently reinforce this understanding.

Where there are competing priorities, always prioritise alignment with NDP IV first. Everything else should support that objective.

If, during implementation, you identify opportunities that strengthen the platform while remaining faithful to the existing architecture, I trust your judgement. Feel free to take the lead where appropriate, provided every enhancement remains consistent with the principles below.

Role-Based Visibility
Different users should see different perspectives of the same underlying information.

However, visibility should remain logical.

For example:

If an MP can inspect the status of a stalled project in Kayunga or Jinja, then an LC I leader responsible for that locality should also be able to access information relevant to that project from their own perspective.

The information shown may differ according to responsibility and authority, but the underlying project should remain the same.

Each role should naturally answer questions such as:

"What am I responsible for?"

"What requires my attention?"

"What action can I take next?"

"What information can I contribute?"

The Golden Rule
The objective is not to create additional pages.

The objective is not to manufacture additional datasets.

The objective is not to increase visual complexity for its own sake.

The objective is to progressively reveal more of the existing information through intuitive navigation, contextual actions, drill-down experiences, guided workflows and role-aware interactions.

Every click should accomplish at least one of the following:

reveal existing information

explain existing information

expose supporting evidence

present the logical next action

allow the user to contribute contextual information

strengthen accountability

improve discoverability

reinforce the government planning workflow

demonstrate role-based oversight

guide decision-making

The finished prototype should feel like a living Government Planning, Monitoring, Evaluation and Intelligence Platform—not merely a collection of static dashboards.

Above all else, preserve the architecture, respect the integrity of the existing data, strengthen the user experience, and maximise the value already contained within the application.
